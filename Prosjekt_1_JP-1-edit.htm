One-dimensional diffusion model for CO2 absorption in the ocean¶

by Simon S. Winther and Øyvind H. Singsaas

In this project, we will be studying two different scenarios for CO2-absorption and diffusion in the ocean – one for shallow areas with constant concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere, and one for the deep ocean with increasing atmospheric concentration of CO2. For both scenarios, we will apply a simplified one-dimensional model of the ocean – a water column - and run simulations over the time. The main components in modelling this, are the solutions to the one-dimensional diffusion equation, eq. (1) in the problem description. While this is a very simple model that neglects many important processes, the hope is that it can tell us something about the behaviour of the ocean as a whole.

Our aim is to use this PDE to model how the concentration of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in the ocean, C(z,t), changes with depth and time, as well as the rate at which CO2 in the atmosphere is absorbed into the ocean. In order to solve this, we need the diffusivity, K(z), and boundary conditions describing the concentration of DIC. The diffusivity changes with the depth, and requires different approximations depending on whether we are modelling shallow waters or the deep ocean.

Below, we have included the code we have used for solving the diffusion equation in the two scenarios, with their respective boundary conditions. In the solution, we first define all relevant constants, and then define the functions that approximate the diffusivity K(z) in the two scenarios. Next, we construct two constant tridiagonal matrixes, L and R, both of which contain both boundary conditions and diffusivity, as instructed in the problem description. Matrices L and R were initially found by discretizing the diffusion equation and by applying the Crank-Nicolson method. In the code we construct these matrixes by creating arrays with each of the diagonal lines in the matrix – using simple for-loops, as they give easy insight to the contents of each matrix. Next, we define the tridiagonal matrix algorithm (TDMA), provided by the "Litt starthjelp til prosjekt 1" post published on Blackboard. We apply this algorithm to solve the final matrix equation, L⋅⋅C (i+1) =V(i+1). Here, C(i+1) represents the concentration at the next time step - which is what we want to solve for. L is the matrix explained above, and V=R⋅⋅Ci+(1/2)(Si + Si+1). Here, R is the other matrix explained above, Ci is the concentration at the current time, and S is a vector dependent on the equilibrium concentration. This matrix equation is constructed and solved in the function called iterate(), applying the TDMA. Finally we define the function simulate(), where we both constructs S, as well as apply iterate to solve the matrix equation with all its components for all given times (using a for-loop through the time array). The implementation of the iterate()-function inside the for-loop in the simulate()-function thus gives us a managable way to solve for any time interval.

We return the results for the concentration of DIC saved in a matrix where each column coresponds to a time point and the rows coresponds to depths. We have chosen a slightly simplified version of simulate() in the first problem, as the equilibrium concentration, and thus S, is constant in this case. The implementations that are specific to only one of the problems will be described as we discuss each of the problem more in detail below.

Problem 1: Response to changing CO2 concentration in shallow areas¶

In the first part of this project, we wanted to study how changes in the atmospheric concentration of CO2 affect shallow areas of the sea. In doing this, we assumed a scenario where:

• There is zero initial concentration of DIC in the ocean.

• The present concentration of 415 ppm CO2 in the atmosphere is constant.

• The depth of the water column is 100 meters.

We ran a 180-day long simulation to illustrate how the concentration of DIC in the sea developed. Below, we show the code that was used to solve the diffusion equation with the appropriate initial conditions, as well as run the simulation. We also construct two different plots to show the results of the simulation, both will be described in detail below, along with further discussion of the results.

When solving the diffusion equation we applied an approximation of the diffusivity K(z) at different depths constructed specifically for shallow waters. This approximation takes into account both how water near the surface is influenced by wind, and how the water near the bottom is influenced by friction between the currents and the sea floor.

a = 6.97e-7 #s/m, 
u = 10 #m/s, average wind speed
k_w = a*u**2  #m/s, (6.97e-5), mass transfer coeff
p_co2 = 415e-6 #atm, partial pressure, CO2
H = 5060 #mol/(m^3atm), proportionality constant
c_eq = H*p_co2 #mol/m^3 (ca. 2.1), equilibrium concentration
secinyear = 365*24*60*60 #seconds in a year

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

# Diffusivity in shallow areas

def K_1(z):
    K_0 = 10**(-3) 
    K_a = 2*10**(-2) 
    z_a = 7 #m
    K_b = 5 * 10**(-2) #m^2
    z_b = 10 #m
    return K_0 + K_a*(z/z_a)*np.exp(-z/z_a) + (K_b*(L-z)/z_b) * np.exp(-(L-z)/z_b)

# Constructing array of diffusivities at depths [0, L] by function K_1.
K_ray = K_1(np.linspace(0, L, N+1))

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
# Constructing tridiagonal matrices

alpha = dt / (2 * dz**2)
gamma = 2*alpha*k_w*dz*(1 - (K_ray[1] - K_ray[0]) / (2*K_ray[0]))
K_merket = np.zeros(N)
for m in range(1, N):
    K_merket[m] = K_ray[m+1] - K_ray[m-1] 

#Construct the three diagonals in the L-matrix (of lengths N, N+1, N), based on stated matrix

L_upper = [-2*alpha*K_ray[0]]
for i in range(1, N):
    L_upper.append((-alpha/4)*K_merket[i] - alpha*K_ray[i])
  
L_mid = [1 + 2*alpha*K_ray[0] + gamma]
for i in range(1, N + 1):
    L_mid.append((1 + 2*alpha*K_ray[i]))
  
L_lower = [(alpha/4)*K_merket[1] - alpha*K_ray[1]]
for i in range(2, N):
    L_lower.append((alpha/4)*K_merket[i] - alpha*K_ray[i])
L_lower.append(-2*alpha*K_ray[N])

#Construct the three diagonals in the R-matrix (of lengths N, N+1, N), based on stated matrix

R_upper = [2*alpha*K_ray[0]]
for i in range(1, N):
    R_upper.append((alpha/4)*K_merket[i] + alpha*K_ray[i])
  
R_mid = [1 - 2*alpha*K_ray[0] - gamma]
for i in range(1, N + 1):
    R_mid.append((1 - 2*alpha*K_ray[i]))
  
R_lower = [(-alpha/4)*K_merket[1] + alpha*K_ray[1]]
for i in range(2, N):
    R_lower.append((-alpha/4)*K_merket[i] + alpha*K_ray[i])
R_lower.append(2*alpha*K_ray[N])

#Place the constructed arrays as diagonals in tridiagonal matrix
L_matrix = diags([L_upper, L_mid, L_lower], offsets = [1, 0, -1]) 
R_matrix = diags([R_upper, R_mid, R_lower], offsets = [1, 0, -1])

#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

#Tridiagonal matrix solver from support code on Blackboard
@jit(nopython = True)
def tdma_solver(a, b, c, d):
    N = len(d)
    c_ = np.zeros(N-1)
    d_ = np.zeros(N)
    x  = np.zeros(N)
    c_[0] = c[0]/b[0]
    d_[0] = d[0]/b[0]
    for i in range(1, N-1):
        c_[i] = c[i]/(b[i] - a[i-1]*c_[i-1])
    for i in range(1, N):
        d_[i] = (d[i] - a[i-1]*d_[i-1])/(b[i] - a[i-1]*c_[i-1])
    x[-1] = d_[-1]
    for i in range(N-2, -1, -1):
        x[i] = d_[i] - c_[i]*x[i+1]
    return x

# Solves Ax = b to find x
def tdma(A, b):
    x = tdma_solver(A.diagonal(-1), A.diagonal(0), A.diagonal(1), b)
    return x
#-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
#Functions doing the simulating

#Iterates and returns C by solving L C_next = V
def iterate(C_vec, R_matrix, S_vec, L_matrix):
    V_matrix = R_matrix.dot(C_vec) + S_vec
    C_vec = tdma(L_matrix, V_matrix)
    
    return C_vec

#Simulates to time T based on the globally defined parameters
def simulate(): 
    C_timeline = [] # Stores arrays of concentrations from t=0 to t=T
    S_vec = np.zeros(N+1)
    S_vec[0] = 2*gamma*c_eq #in this problem, S_vec is constant
    C_vec = np.zeros(N+1) # Starting concentration is zero
    for t in range(int(T/dt)):
        C_timeline.append(C_vec)
        C_vec = iterate(C_vec, R_matrix, S_vec, L_matrix)    
    return C_timeline # [timepoint][coordinate]

Highest and lowest concentration of DIC over time (Figure 1)¶

We first used the results from the simulation to find both maximum and minimum values for concentration of DIC in the ocean, for all time steps. Figure 1 shows the plot of these values against time, as well as the constant equilibrium concentration (C_eq). This illustrates how both the lowest and highest concentration values converge towards the equilibrium concentration as time increases. Thus, with the given conditions and assumption, the system will approach homogenous distribution of DIC over time. As the system reaches homogenous concentration equal to the equilibrium concentration throughout, the flux of CO2 through the surface will approach zero.

Concentration as a function of depth throughout the time interval (Figure 2)¶

In figure 2, we plot the concentration of DIC in the water against the depth for different times. On the first axis, we have concentration of DIC, and on the second axis we have depth z (z = 0 at surface, z = -100 at bottom). Each line then coresponds to the consentration of DIC at a given time. From this, we can see how the concentration changes over time – first increasing near the surface due to mass transfer, and then increasing downwards through the water column dependent on the diffusivity. By the shape of the lines, you can see how the diffusivity is not constant but varies with the depth, affected by wind near the surface and friction near the sea bottom. The concentration near the bottom lags behind throughout much of the process. However, as time progresses, you can see how the concentration approaches homogeneity throughout the entire water column, reflecting how the concentration is approaching the equilibrium concentration. After 180 days, by the end of the simulation, you can see how the concentration is very close to constant throughout the entire water column, which illustrates how equilibrium is almost reached.

How small yearly changes in atmospheric CO2-level affect shallow waters¶

What can this simulation tell us about the beaviour of the ocean when there is a smaller, more realistic yearly increase of CO2 in the atmosphere? In the scenario discussed above, the DIC concentration in water at 100 meters depth reached the constant equilibrium concentration and “caught up” to the atmospheric CO2 levels in approximately 6 months- a time scale we consider to be small when assessing questions about the climate.

In the initial conditions of this simulation the DIC conentration is zero and it has to catch up to C_eq = 2.1 [mol/m3], a much greater difference than we would expect in the real world. How will the concentration of DIC in the water column keep up with this change? Considering how small this yearly change in C_eq will be compared to the gap between zero initial concentration of DIC and the initial C_eq that was applied in the scenario above, as well as how rapidly the equilibrium was reached with the large initial gap – it seems reasonable to expect that the water at 100 meters depth will stay close to “in sync” with the changes in the atmosphere.

The diffusion of DIC over 10 years (Figure 3)¶

Figure 3 shows the result from simulating 10 years of CO2 absorption from 2020 to 2030. One line corresponds to one time instance during the simulation. On the first axis we have the concentration and on the second axis the depth z. The full lines correspond to t = 0, 2.5 years, 5 years and 10 years. The dotted lines shows the equilibrium concentration at the time corresponding to its color. The amount of CO2 in the atmosphere is modelled with a linear function 415 ppm + 2.3 pp/year. The diffusion is here a sigmoid function dependent on depth.

The upper layers, down to about a hundred meters, are almost at equilibrium with the atmosphere. After 10 years, depths below thousand meters are still unaffected by the increase of CO2 in the atmosphere, but we can see the increase in concentration in the upper layers slowly affecting the deeper parts. This comes down to the function used to describe how the diffusivity changes with depth.

The function describing the diffusion has a substantial drop-of around z = 100 meters as the diffusivity goes from K_1 to K_0 (illustrated in appendix figure A.1). This models the effect illustrated in Figure 2 in the problem description, where turbulence helps the CO2 mix into the water. This effect dies of as we look at greater ocean depths. In these deeper layers of the ocean, the diffusion (as defined in the problem description) relies more on diffusion on the molecular level, since the water is not as turbulent as in the upper layers.

Estimate of annual DIC mass absorbed by the oceans (Figure 4)¶

Figure 4 shows the increase in dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) from 2020 to 2030. First we integrate the concentration [mol/m3] with respect to depth, which gives us the concentration per surface area of ocean [mol/m2]. We then multiply this by the total area of the global oceans, 360⋅⋅1012 [m2], to get the total amount of mols absorbed. Since all the DIC molecules contain one carbon atom, we simply multiply by the atomic mass of carbon, 12 grams per mole. Doing this for the period 2020 to 2030 gives us the results ploted in Figure 4.

To get the average yearly absorption rate of CO2 we need only consider the total mass of DIC in 2020 and 2030. We take the difference and divide it by 10 years. This gives a value of 11.89⋅⋅1015 gram per year.

As a reference value, Gruber et. al (2019) found that the ocean absorb ca. 2.5 ⋅⋅1015 g CO2 produced by human activities per year. Our model gave an absorption rate of approximately 12⋅⋅1015 g per year, a factor 4.8 times bigger than the result presented by Gruber et. al (2019). Considering the simplicity of our model, we regard this as fairly close.

In this regard, we will now look at some simplifications in our model and how they have affected our result qualitatively.

Simplifications made:¶

Assuming homogeneous oceans¶

By using a 1 dimensional model we assume horizontal homogeneity for the entire ocean. This is a big simplification. The ocean temperatures are obviously not the same at all locations on earth and locations with higher ocean temperatures would be able to absorb less CO2. We have also assumed the wind speed to be the same at all locations. This is also not true and would effect the diffusivity at the surface and at depths affected by the turbulence caused by it.

Ocean currents transport massive amounts of water between the ocean layers and between geographical locations. At the locations where the water is transported from the surface to greater depths, for example at the poles, the diffusivity would be greater. Our model does not include this, as we assume the diffusivity to follow the same function at all locations. The currents also change the concentration of DIC between locations, which we also do not include, as we simply look at one isolated water column. We would expect that including this in a model would increase the amount of CO2 absorbed, since it could be regarded as turbulence in the system.

CO2 absorbing life forms¶

Another complex factor that we choose to ignore is how life in the ocean affect concentrations of DIC. Life forms such as phytoplankton, algae, and seaweed use CO2 through photosynthesis, while zooplankton, fish and other animals produce CO2.
